Research (2022-ongoing)

Trust in Automation

In this research project, titled "Examining inconsistencies in the use of the Trust in Automated Systems Scale (Jian et al., 2000)," I delve into the multifaceted concept of trust in automation. This investigation is particularly timely as the fusion of sophisticated technology with complex work environments amplifies the need for a deeper understanding of human-automation interactions.

My Role:

As a fellow researcher embarking on a journey in the field of trust in automated systems, my role was pivotal in dissecting the Trust in Automated Systems Scale's application across various studies. This endeavor involved a comprehensive analysis of over a thousand scholarly articles, identifying how the scale was adapted to fit different research contexts. My fresh perspective and keen analytical skills contributed to uncovering the nuances of scale modifications and their implications on the accuracy of trust measurement in automated systems.

Project Highlights:
Objective:

The aim was to scrutinize the application of the Jian scale in measuring trust in automation, highlighting inconsistencies and proposing a path toward standardization.

Methodology:

The research entailed a thorough review of peer-reviewed articles that cited the Jian scale, evaluating how it was modified and implemented in different studies. This process illuminated the diverse ways researchers have adapted the scale to suit specific automated systems and study requirements.

Key Insights:

The investigation revealed a variety of modifications to the Jian scale, ranging from minor wording changes to significant alterations. These findings underscore the complexity of measuring trust in automation and the need for a more uniform approach to ensure the reliability of trust assessments.

Impact and Contribution:

This project stands as a testament to the intricate relationship between humans and automated technologies. By highlighting the critical role of trust measurement in designing user-centric automated systems, the research contributes valuable insights to both the academic community and the industry. My participation as a fellow researcher not only enhanced my understanding of trust in automation but also positioned me as an emerging voice in this evolving field of study.

UX project 2022

Grocery Delivery UAV

This project embarked on designing a state-of-the-art, user-centric workstation for Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) operators, focusing on enhancing the usability and efficiency for pilots, payload operators, and navigators.

Design Objectives
Interfaces:

The core aim was to develop interfaces that are inherently intuitive for UAV operators, reducing the cognitive load and streamlining operational workflows.

Critical Feature Integration:

A significant innovation was the "Return to Base" feature, designed to trigger under critical conditions like connectivity loss, ensuring the UAV's safe return and highlighting the workstation's reliability.

Development
Advanced UX Design:

The project was a platform for applying and enhancing advanced User Experience (UX) design principles, focusing on creating interfaces that are both functional and user-friendly.

User Research:

A deep dive into user research allowed for a thorough understanding of UAV operators' needs and preferences, informing the design process with real-world insights.

Ergonomic Interface Creation:

The emphasis was also on ergonomics, ensuring that the workstation design conforms to human factors principles, thereby reducing operator fatigue and improving efficiency.

Team Collaboration and Innovation:

The project was a collaborative effort involving a multidisciplinary team, where I played a key role in fostering teamwork, communication, and innovation. Working closely with designers, engineers, and user research specialists, we leveraged diverse perspectives to address design challenges and create a workstation that truly stands out in terms of usability and reliability.

Research (05/2023)

    Digital Note-Taking Affordances Effect on Cognitive Workload

This project focused on the educational technology, we explored the impact of different digital note-taking methods on students' cognitive workload. Our research scrutinized the use of tablets in classrooms, comparing the cognitive effects of note-taking with a stylus, on-screen keyboard, and attached keyboard. Contrary to expectations, our findings indicated that the mode of digital note-taking did not significantly influence cognitive workload. The collaborative nature of this project sharpened my teamwork and research skills. Working alongside my peers, I gained proficiency in the design and execution of a structured study, data collection, and critical analysis. We integrated findings from Segal (2011), which highlighted the benefits of tablets in educational settings, to contextualize our results within the broader landscape of classroom technology. The teamwork aspect taught me the value of diverse perspectives and the strength of collective effort in achieving research goals. This experience has equipped me with a strong foundation in collaborative inquiry and the application of empirical evidence to support academic findings, preparing me for future ventures in the field of educational technology research.
